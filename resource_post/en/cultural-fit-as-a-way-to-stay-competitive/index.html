Cultural Fit As A Way to Stay Competitive
PostedWed 13, Apr 2016

On the morning of April 7, Seedlink held an informative HR seminar Cultural Fit As A Way To Stay Competitive. Our guest speakers for this sharing session are experts in the related realm, and they offered a new focus on recruiting, engaging and transforming talents into advocates for greater corporate success amidst the challenges of a slowing Chinese economy.This article outlines the gist of these topics.

How Company Culture Can Define Recruitment Success

Robin Young, Founder & CEO of Seedlink

Company culture refers to the assumptions and norms shared by the employees –- it is the glue that holds an organization together. Meanwhile, cultural fit is how much someone will reflect and/or be able to adapt to the core beliefs, attitudes, and behaviors that make up the organization. Studies have consistently shown that employees who fit well with their organizations, colleagues, and supervisors had greater job satisfaction. They also showed superior job performance and more likely to be loyal to the organisation. As such, it is important to examine the extent of cultural fit of a candidate during the process of hiring.

Hence, in order for the hiring team to be able to start measuring candidates’ culture fit, the culture of the organization must first be defined – its values, goals, and practices — and then weave this understanding into the hiring process.

While noting that most companies tend to neglect this particular aspect of recruiting, Robin argued for otherwise and stressed that culture fit is one of the core ingredients to a successful recruitment. How well a person fits into the culture is deeply intertwined with the character of the individual, and one accurate way to assess personality is through the language he/she uses to express thoughts and perspective. As such, with help of Artificial Intelligence and behavior analytics, Seedlink can accurately make benchmark of a corporate culture and measure the extent of cultural fit of the candidates.

12 New Rules Of Engagement: Redefining The Employee Engagement Strategy

David Litteken, Managing Director for Asia Pacific Japan & China for BI Worldwide

Employee engagement can build a conducive environment for all members of an organisation to feel an enhanced sense of their own well-being, and hence motivated to do their best job. In order to create the conditions in which employees can better realise their potential and dedicate more of their energy to the work, a company should build a foundation of trust, integrity, two-way commitment and communication between the organisation and its members. It is an approach that increases the chance of business success.

David shared invaluable insights into Employee Engagement with these rules of engagement:

  1. My manager understands me
  2. Positive outlook about the future
  3. Compensated fairly
  4. Low stress and work life balance
  5. Cool and exciting place to work
  6. Get the information needed to do the job well
  7. Work connects with a larger purpose
  8. Excited about what comes next
  9. Recognized for the best work
  10. Part of a cohesive team
  11. Opportunity to lead on projects
  12. Have a strong sense of accomplishment

Following these rules, he had established track records of global employee engagement where there was a 19% increase in senior leadership engagement, 23% increase in employee engagement i.e. overall satisfaction and the realization of a statistically significant reduction in turnover, generating substantial cost savings.

Building Your Culture By Transforming Employees Into Your Brand Advocates

Sina Farzaneh, Founder of Advocacy and Pullpath

Sina Farzaneh, Founder of Advocacy and Pullpath, talking about how to build unique company culture.

Advocacy is defined as the recommendation for a particular cause or policy. In marketing, advocacy is the king of the food chain, above loyalty, trail, consideration and awareness. There are 3 pillars to employee advocacy: people (tribes), objectives (topics) and rewards (incentives). Secondly, the process of employee advocacy has to first go through Reach, Sales, Trust, Content, Engagement, and then reach the ultimate objective —Referral, where the employees genuinely recommend a product or service out of personal belief rather than tangible incentive. Lastly, Rewards has to undergo Progress, Contents and collect Responses. A regular feedback loop is essential to ensure that everyone is kept engaged throughout the entire progress and also a communication platform that allows for agile adjustments to evolving needs.
